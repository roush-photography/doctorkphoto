What does the future hold for the Gulf states after Deepwater Horizon? Is full recovery possible? The truth is we don't really know. The answer would seem to be a definite maybe, though it will be a slow, painful process. The road to recovery may be glimpsed, perhaps, by looking at Ixtoc, in the Gulf of Campeche off the coast of Mexico, the second largest spill in history at four million barrels. This took place in 1979.

Local reefs around Veracruz are still showing effects, 35 years later. On the lagoon side the reef is gray and dead, and the sea floor is lithified (turned into stone) oil, like some new geological novelty. On the ocean side it's a different story. There wave energy, oxygen, sunlight, and the Gulf's oil digesting bacteria cleared most visible evidence of the spill in a comparatively short time, years, not decades. The lesson for the Gulf states, and the world for that matter, is fairly clear. When oil is entrapped in low oxygen sediments, as in a quiet lagoon or a marsh, or on the deep ocean bottom, it can hang around indefinitely. The real consequences of this may never be known, but they can't be good, or even neutral, which leaves only one conclusion.

So what about fisheries? The 1978 Amoco Cadiz spill may shed some light on this. Even in the turbulent, highly oxygenated waters off the coast of France, where this spill occurred, it took seven years for the marine life and oyster farms to recover. Things in Mexico weren't so good. Ixtoc devastated local fisheries. Populations of fish, shrimp, conch, and oysters were completely wiped out. In a split second fishermen were unemployed, indefinitely, their future as murky as raw crude. Most gave up and found new work after being hungry long enough. For many families a long tradition of fishing came to a sad end. What about the few who stuck it out? It was 20 years before catches returned to normal. (next photograph)
