The presence of toxic chemicals in our environment
is one of the grimmest discoveries of the industrial era.
President Jimmy Carter

"Never have a people spent so much money on so many expensive things as Americans are today." With this simple but prophetic statement in the 1950s, Fortune Magazine proclaimed the modern era of consumerism. Other magazines (Life, Look etc) would follow suit, and advertise ad nauseum to fan the "material" flames.

The 1950s witnessed other sweeping changes to our lives as well, with booms in housing and road construction. A new problem called "traffic congestion" was making the news in our cities. It was suggested that one day it might lead to traffic controls, or even "stop" lights. The summer vacation became affordable for many Americans and they sped in record numbers to our national parks. Federal agencies engaged in massive reshaping of the land with dam building, wetland drainage, flood control, and river dredging, which fueled the greatest economic growth in world history. It seemed the more we conquered nature the richer we got. A popular advertising slogan of the times was "Progress is our most important product." Indeed life in the USA was "progress" in the fast lane. Boom, boom, boom.

But there was a catch to all this progress. Seems whenever we play we have to pay, and in the 1950s we were playing with atomic bombs like they were toys, "testing" (a nice word for exploding) in the Nevada desert, on uninhabited South Pacific islands, and other places that didn't matter much. Or so we thought. Come to find out we had riddled the atmosphere with radioactive fallout that didn't vanish into thin air after all. Rather it floated around the globe and settled, not just over there, but here, in our own backyards. The experts seemed to have overlooked this possibility. Oops. When Strontium 90 was found in cow's milk, a staple in baby formulas, we began to talk test-ban treaty with Russia. President Kennedy signed the treaty in 1962 and considered it the high point of his presidency. That treaty is still in effect.


Even in wilderness areas, like the West Elk in Colorado in the photograph, man's toxic impact is felt. There are over 20,000 closed mines in the state, many of them leaking heavy metals (mercury, arsenic, lead, cadmium) and acid into mountain streams. Such streams are often devoid of fish or other life-forms. In August of 2015, less than a week after I visited the area, three millions gallons of this toxic brew poured out the abandoned Gold King mine and into the Animas River, which runs past Silverton and Durango on its way to the Colorado River. The EPA had offered to clean up the site with Superfund money but the local jurisdiction refused, fearing such an effort would scare tourists away. The Animas River has been responsible for 10% of the white water rafting revenue in Colorado. That just might change.
