But this fabulous hominid was not content to merely adapt to a changing world. He was about to break the rules, in fact to shatter them to smithereens. About 10,000 years ago man began to do something else that had not been done (the first was walking upright). He began to modify rather than adapt to his environment, making the world fit his needs rather than the other way around. Instead of just hunting and gathering, man learned to domesticate plants and animals. By about 6,000 BCE, in a fertile crescent around the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, in present-day Iraq, the transition was complete. For the first time anywhere in the world man was totally sedentary, living within an artificial ecosystem that he constructed and totally depended upon. This was the beginning of agriculture and the seeds of a revolution. Man was now a farmer and a shepherd, and on his way to becoming a city slicker.

The Agricultural Revolution was mankind's real "giant leap," the single most important chapter in the incomparable biography of Homo sapiens. Hunting and gathering groups were egalitarian. They had few possessions, the idea of land ownership was completely alien to them, and goods vital to the group as a whole (for example food) were regarded as belonging to everyone and shared. It took agriculture to change all this.

From the simple agricultural villages of southwest Asia would come the world we know today-nations with cities and hierarchical, militaristic societies, ruled by religious and political elite with enormous powers over the common citizen. Societies that were broadly egalitarian were replaced by ones with distinct classes and huge differences in wealth. The ability to support people not engaged in food production with an agricultural surplus became the basis for all subsequent cultural and technological advances by humans. And with that we have grazed, plowed, and settled the entire planet, and even placed footprints on the moon in 1969.

Truly a job well done! And it was all made possible by a farmer.
