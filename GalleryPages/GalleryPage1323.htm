There was a dramatic illustration of our complacency toward the oceans during the International Geophysical Year of 1957. Oceanographers in the U.S. proudly announced to the world that they were going to dump radioactive wastes into the ocean and check out the effects. And they were serious! Tons of the radioactive goo were stored in 55 gallon drums, similar to ones you might see rusting in any industrial yard. They were hauled on barges to an area near the Faralon Islands (which happen to be near San Francisco), off the California coast, and dumped into the drink. Many of them didn't sink so U.S. Navy gunners shot them full of holes to let water in. And, of course, uranium and plutonium out. By the time the project was stopped in the early nineties hundreds of thousands of drums lay rusting on the ocean floor around the United States. And we weren''t alone in this fiasco. Many other developed nations enthusiastically joined in.

And too, we've been to the moon and outer reaches of the solar system, yet still know relatively little about our oceans. For example the largest creature that ever lived was not a dinosaur at all. It IS the blue whale and, though endangered, she still swims the seas. The largest of these leviathans ever recorded was harpooned by whalers off South Georgia Island, in the Southern Ocean, in 1926. She measured 111 feet long and a whopping 400,000 pounds, twice as big as any dinasaur.

To bring these numbers into better focus, a blue whale calf can weigh three tons (adult rhino size) at birth and grow 200 pounds a day on mom's milk. And as British filmmaker David Attenborough famously said, an adult blue whale's "tongue weighs as much as an elephant, its heart is the size of a Volkswagon, and you could swim in its largest blood vessels." And yet despite this "wonder" that still lives among us we know very little about it other than measurements.

 

I love visiting out of the way places where few have been, and the whaling station at Grytviken, established at South Georgia in 1904, is certainly one of those. The great explorer Ernest Shackleton began his fateful Trans-Antarctic Expedition here in 1914 and is buried under the white cross. This is where the massive blue whale mentioned in the text was taken. About 25, 000 whales were harvested in waters off South Georgia before the station closed in 1966. The whalers were then unemployed. Someone forgot to tell them that if you kill all the whales there won't be any left to make new whales. The mathematical formula for this is X-X=0, where X is the number of whales in the ocean AND the number you take out of the ocean. Admittedly these can be hard concepts to grasp.
