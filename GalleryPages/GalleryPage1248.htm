Alaska is itself an unusual state.
Fareed Zakaria

The twin-engine prop jet takes off from Fairbanks on a foul day with nine passengers aboard. We're bound for Kaktovik, on Barter Island in the Arctic Ocean, at the edge of the known universe. Getting anywhere in Alaska is half the adventure, if not the fun. We have 400 miles of trackless tundra and Brooks Range to overfly in roughly two hours, no pun intended.

I'm settling in for a snooze when I notice out the window a total whiteout. I can't even see the wing tips, a few feet away. I know there are instruments in the cockpit, but not having a sense of spatial orientation challenges my composure. A short time later the engine slows, then backfires. The plane lurches, then banks as if trying to avoid something-perhaps a mountain or another plane? We fly an additional 30 seconds in more or less a straight line of continual descent, still in the whiteout. I am pretty certain by now that we are all going to die. To prove it the ground makes an abrupt appearance, very close. The plane drops with a suddenness that makes hair stand up straight. Then BANG. Contact! We bounce and skid, then taxi down a runway that miraculously is there, like a gift from god. The sign says Yukon Flats. The pilot says we have some mail to deliver (now he tells us). So much for my snooze. Welcome to the outback of Alaska.
