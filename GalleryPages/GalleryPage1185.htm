The Vietnam War was arguably the most traumatic experience for the United States in the twentieth century. That is indeed a grim distinction in a span that included two world wars, the assassinations of two presidents and the resignation of another, the Great Depression, the Cold War, and racial unrest.
Donald Goldstein-"The Vietnam War"

Photography has been called every man's art form. The power of photography to influence opinion has been understood for a long time, dating back to before the Civil War. And no genre of photography has done it better than photojournalism, especially in turbulent times.

This was never more apparent than during the Vietnam War. Three images from that war were emblazoned in the consciousness of the world during the sixties and seventies, and helped bring the controversial conflict to a halt.

In 1963 the Catholic regime of President Diem of South Vietnam was repressive to Buddhists, who comprised a majority of the country's population. In protest, Quang Duc, a Buddhist monk, drove his Austin from the Imperial City of Hue to a busy intersection in Saigon, the capital. He then got out the vehicle and assumed a lotus position in the heavy traffic. A fellow monk doused him with gasoline, whereupon Duc struck a match to himself. His burning image was flashed around the world. In response, President Kennedy withdrew support for Diem, who was then assassinated as his government toppled.

Tension mounted as the surprise Tet Offensive began in 1968. Saigon's police chief, Ngoc Loan, was on alert to keep Viet Cong guerrillas out of the city. Loan executed Van Lam, a prisoner, in front of An Quang Pagoda as photographer Eddie Adams snapped a Pulitzer Prize winning photo. This image helped to turn the tide of public opinion against the war, not just in the United States, but around the world. Loan claimed Van Lam had killed Americans and Vietnamese, while others argued that he was just a foot soldier, following orders, who did not deserve to be executed. (next photo)
