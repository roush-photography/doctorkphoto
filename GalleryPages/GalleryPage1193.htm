The rules of life dictate that a species adapt to an inconstant world, or else. This is true for all life, all the time. Or is it?

Once upon a time there was a crafty quadruped, a hunter-gatherer that swung his way out of the African jungle and onto the savanna. Unlike the jungle, his new world had few climbing trees. Our prehuman ancestor thus decided to adapt and become a biped-that is, to walk around on just his hind legs. A new species was born whose hands were free to do other useful things. This was truly a giant step for early man. Homo erectus, by the very act of standing erect and walking, changed the future of the Earth and all its life-forms forever.

He did it initially by following all of Darwin's rules to become the supreme adaptor, the best there ever was. Using his increasingly complex brain and remarkable hands, with their grasping thumbs, he built shelters, crafted tools and weapons, and learned to use fire for heat and cooking. Thus would Homo erectus, and finally Homo sapiens, become the most successful species of all time, the only one to adapt to every ecological niche on the planet. An impressive resume, no doubt!

 

There are very few strictly hunter-gatherer societies today. Examples are the Hadza of Tanzania; the Spinifex people of the Great Victorian Desert in Australia; and the Sentinelese tribe of the Andaman Islands in the Indian Ocean, who are openly hostile to anything Western. The Dani of New Guinea, pictured above, are more neolithic, having incorporated agriculture and many modern implements into their way of life. (next photo)
