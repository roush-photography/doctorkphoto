Like most of the world's charismatic megavertebrates (whale, elephant, lion, tiger), the sea turtle is in a race for survival. Once so abundant that Columbus described the Caribbean as "full of floating rocks," all eight species are now threatened or endangered. There is a single solitary reason for this plight and that is man, his activities and his indifference.

The sea turtle's dilemma demonstrates just how dominant man's dominion over the earth has become. Some turtle species are shallow water. Unfortunately for them so too are man and his nets. But even deep water species are endangered. They must periodically surface for air and likely as not they will encounter something human along the way. And the sea turtle is pelagic, or open ocean, which again would seem like a good thing. Yet females must come to shore one night a year to lay eggs. On land the sluggish turtles don't have a chance. Imagine then how the eggs fare.

For this ponderous giant life starts out dangerous and then gets worse. Every stroke of the flipper seems to dodge or weave some hazard along life's treacherous way. Eggs are ingested as food and (you guessed it!) aphrodesiac. Hatchlings are gulped down like hors d'oeuvres by birds and mammals. Nesting beaches are buried beneath condominiums. The few turtles that do make it to adulthood can be killed for food, ground up by dredges, plowed by propellers, poisoned by pollution, strangled by plastic, or entangled (by the hundreds of thousands) in fishermen's nets.

Where have all the floating rocks gone? Gone to indifference, every one.
