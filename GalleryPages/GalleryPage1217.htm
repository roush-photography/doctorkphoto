I was raised a few miles up the road from the Gulf of Mexico and spent a lot of time there, vacationing with my family. I fished, crabbed, swam, and explored miles of deserted beaches. The Gulf seemed infinite back then, chock full of speckled trout, red snapper, blue crab, and mysterious creatures of the deep that washed ashore and never ceased to amaze. In those days the beaches were buttery colored dunes anchored by sea oats and patrolled only by gulls and pelicans. The salty surf teemed with a cornucopia of shells, of every size, shape, and color.

Then in decades, not eons, it all changed. The bounty began to disappear before our very eyes. Before we knew it the monk seal was gone, the manatee endangered. By the end of the 20th century the Gulf had been nearly depleted of apex species, a result of overfishing. Populations of shark, swordfish, marlin, tuna, turtle, and whale were down 50 to 90 percent. Half of all coral reefs were seriously damaged or dead! Miles and miles of marsh and sea grass meadow were converted to malls, marinas, and motels. Oil rigs by the thousands were strewn about the coast and continental shelf. And instead of nurturing the Gulf of Mexico with nutrients the Mississippi River poisoned it with toxic chemicals. Poets penned odes to the "Sea of Sacrifice," lamenting the price paid for the modern world.

The Gulf is in deep trouble. But alas, a few bright spots still flicker out there. Some areas have managed to escape the trawler. In spite of the massive environmental degradation, there is still an asonishing diversity of life waiting on tighter fishing controls to repopulate. And there are even a few much needed sanctuaries, around Florida, Mexico, and Cuba, and more being discussed. These provide hope for the Gulf, and for us all.

 

Japan is one of the nations most responsible for the worldwide collapse of commercial fish species, through harmful and unsustainable fishing practices. And marine mammals aren't spared either. The Japanese continue whaling despite a worldwide moratorium declared in the 1980s by the International Whaling Commission. Whale meat is consumed only because it is a status symbol, an exotic delicacy in elite social circles.
