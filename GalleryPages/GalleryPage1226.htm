From the redwood forests
To the Gulf Stream waters
This land was made for you and me
Woodie Guthrie

This is the primeval forest. Nothing can prepare you for it. No matter how many times you read about it or see it in pictures, the giant redwood forest will still take your breath away. Your mind will shut down, unable to comprehend anything of this magnitude, and for several moments you will stand there, lost for words and awestruck. You will wonder how anything on earth could be so magnificent.

It has been my good fortune to have gazed upon many of the world's great panoramas-Grand Canyon, Machu Picchu, Iguazu Falls-and in my opinion, the view and cathedral-like silence of a grove of giant redwoods compares with any.

The coastal redwoods of Northern California grow within a few miles of the world's largest ocean and spend much of their lives shrouded in fog as a result. For the photographer this is mostly a good thing. It not only adds a sense of mystery to the forest, but it softens the light and reduces glare in the godbeams of this underworld.

But this is ridiculous! The fog is everywhere, threaded among the trees, adrift on meadows, and rising like steam from the asphalt. It reminds me of pea soup, so thick I can part it or play with it, which I am in no mood to do. I feel damp and dispirited but take solace in knowing that all fog must lift sometime, even this one. But when?

The path is slippery from condensation, but the air feels clean, with a chill. I shuffle cautiously forward, ever deeper into the gloomy silence. The fog is getting thicker if that is possible, the world around me erased. Visibility in this white soup is measured in inches. All I hear are my own muffled footsteps and the sound of dewdrops falling like light rain.

Finally, a miracle in the dell. The fog parts ever so slightly from above, admitting a golden beam through the canopy to reveal the surrounding woodlands. And then it opens like a theatre curtain being drawn apart. I force myself not to shout as I try to comprehend the surroundings. My eyes are pulled upward, along the godbeams, and I see I am enveloped by the world's tallest trees as they reach to the giddying heights of heaven. I am agog at the scale of the place, uncertain where to place my gaze. One thing I am very certain of, however, is that no other forest will ever look quite the same again.
