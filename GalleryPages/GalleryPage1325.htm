Beauty is in the eyes of the beholder.
Margaret Hungerford

The elephant seal is the "Rodney Dangerfield" of the ocean, garnering little respect in human circles. Not so the great white shark, fighter jet of the deep, or bottlenose dolphin, the brainchild. And all acknowledge his highness the blue whale, grand leviathan of the high seas.

But what about this grandest of seals? Why no respect? Well, for starters, it's the comic physique. The words ponderous and homely come to mind. Even the elephant seal's trunk is just wrong, like it's been smashed. It's the subject of jokes. Not so his African namesake. His is the right trunk, a wonder of wonders. Why so?

It turns out to be a problem of perspective. Through our eyes an elephant seal on a beach looks totally out of place. No smooth curves or rippling sinew here. Submerged, however, it's a different story. In his own element our seal is not only the epitome of grace and agility, he's also a deep diver and long- distance swimmer.

After two months of turf wars on rocky beaches around the world, during which time they mostly fight and make up, the beachmasters and their harems slither into the sea, where they spend the rest of the year. Once underwater, an almost magical transformation takes place. These pinnipeds will venture halfway round the world in pursuit of fish and squid, and dive a mile deep for up to two hours. That's deeper than a submarine! They then surface to breathe, recover in minutes, and dive again. And again. These feats are made possible by large capacity lungs, an extraordinary blood volume, and a shifty circulation. Thus plenty of oxygen is shunted just where and when it is needed while the seal is holding its breath for hours. So extraordinary are these seal-a-thons that scientists are attaching devices to the beasts to gather data on climate change. So forget the comic nose and physique, this superhero is helping to save the planet.

 

This so called "seal diving reflex" taught us a lesson or two about ourselves. The human fetus spends nine months exposed to extremely low oxygen pressures, low enough to kill an adult. And during labor it gets worse as each uterine contraction decreases blood flow through the placenta. The fetus survives just like the seal, with a large blood volume, and by preferentially shunting blood flow to brain, heart, and adrenals, while limiting flow to other organs that need less.
