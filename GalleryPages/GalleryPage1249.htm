Three cheers for Alaska, they've got 24-hour freaking bear delivery.
Note to self: Nuke Alaska
Mykle Hansen

After the mail drop the remainder of the flight to Kaktovik is anticlimactic. It is mid-September, autumn in the far north. The sky is thick with cloud cover which by now is spitting snow on this remote village of 200 Inuit. The locals are hunkering down for another long winter, which is how they spend about 75 percent of their lives.

Each September an enduring ritual is played out on this half-frozen stage. Traditional whaling crews go out in open boats on a hunt that is sacred to their culture. They will attempt to fill subsistence quotas established by the U.S. or Canadian governments. For the village of Kaktovik the quota is three bowhead (also known as "right") whales. The meat and blubber give the people protein and fat they need to survive another Arctic winter. And the hunt strengthens ties to the past and bonds within the Inuit community.

The hunt does something else. It has given rise to a fledgling tourist industry on this godforsaken island. After a whale is butchered the remainder of the carcass is dozed onto a lonely spit of land fittingly called the boneyard. Nearly a hundred polar bears are attracted to the area to scavenge this bonanza. In turn, about a hundred visitors assemble to view and photograph the bears, and to soak up the minus five star luxury of the Waldo Arms, Kaktovik's only hotel. Not exactly Disney, but it's a beginning.

 

The local guide drives his motorboat onto the snowy banks of Barter Island so five photographers can sit awhile and "shoot" the crowd of polar bears gathered in the distance, about 30 or 40 of them. One of the bears begins a slow and ponderous march toward the boat from about 300 yards away. Hundreds of photos are taken as the bear approaches. Finally he is close enough that the photographers stop taking pictures and start fidgeting. The guide says nothing, then looks away and takes a long drag on his cigarette. Finally, just as the bear is about to climb aboard, he nonchalantly reaches for the key, starts the engine, and puts it in reverse. As noted in the photograph, the bear is disappointed. We are neither disappointed or amused.
