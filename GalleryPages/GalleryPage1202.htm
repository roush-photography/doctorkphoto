Easter Island is the most remote inhabited place on earth. It lies 2300 miles west of South America and 1300 miles east of Pitcairn Island, home to the nearest neighbors. This lonely, tiny outpost is an obscure chapter in the human story that just might reveal the way to grace for our species.

The Dutch "discovered" the island on Easter Sunday of 1722, thus the name. What they found were embattled "Stone Age" clans engaged in constant warfare and practicing slavery and cannibalism. Most lived in caves hacked out of hillsides. These natives had no clue about the great monuments and statuary that lay strewn about their tiny cosmos, which told of a glorious past, and for which the island is now famous.

There are no written or oral records so the mystery of Easter Island has had to be unveiled by archaeologists sifting through the soil, petroglyphs, and pollen. The truth comes hard. Sweeping conclusions come and go with each new discovery. What follows is a simplistic account of what happened to Polynesia's most advanced statement. There are many blank spaces to fill.
