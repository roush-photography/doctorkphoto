Who will speak for planet earth?
Carl Sagan

We humans are totally dependent on a healthy planet, and yet mother nature is so generous that most of us live our lives without giving her a second thought. At the most basic level, all of our foodstuff comes from nature. Only plants can manufacture food from "raw materials." But plants and animals throughout history have also provided man with clothing, shelter, tools, fuel, timber, oils, dyes, and myriad other products. It is estimated that 25% of modern medicines are derived from plants as are all high-yield food crops responsible for the recent "green revolution."

We have only just begun to tap the potential genetic and bioengineering benefits that might be derived from even the most obscure of species, few of which have been adequately studied. For example, the naked mole rat of Africa, a blind, underground, hairless, and (some would say) hideous creature that lives the human equivalent of a thousand years and does not get cancer! We know little else about this amazing species. What other secrets are out there, burrowing around in the dirt?

Nature and biodiversity also exert a profound spiritual and aesthetic impact on people, a benefit hard to put a dollar value on, so it is mostly ignored in political circles. The presence of life animates our world and enhances our emotional response to it. The complexity of the aesthetic response is suggested by the variety of inspirations, from a mountain stream in the Great Smokies to a hummingbird in California to the wonder of the Grand Canyon in Arizona. These experiences evoke intense feelings in us-feelings of joy, rapture, and even transcendence. Such mystical events are among the most sought-after by our species and are often provoked by the sudden insight that all things are one. From such epiphanies came the enlightenment of Christ and Muhammad as they wandered in the desert, and Lord Buddha as he meditated under the Bodhi tree. Some things are priceless!

 

For several years this Anna's hummingbird has returned to Morro Bay, California after a few months of soaking up the sun in Baja, Mexico. He "maps" his territory by flashing this remarkable display, designed to keep other males away, flying in a circuit to the same tree branches, day after day, year after year, to do so. The purple neck and fanned tail last for a split second, disappear, and then he's off to the next branch to repeat the performance. He doesn't seem to mind photographers at all. They keep coming back year after year, following him around, and trying to capture the ephemeral beauty. It took two full mornings of frenzied shooting before I was finally successful.
