Dan O’Brien (on the Missouri River)

The Dust Bowl changed everything about farming and life on the Great Plains. Both the Canadian and U.S. governments became involved to help stabilize the situation. The U.S. Agricultural Adjustment Act of 1933 initiated a program of subsidies that is still in use. Farmers are payed not to plant in order to protect the soil and to keep prices up. Their initial resistance to this blatant "government interference" was short lived. Today, a substantial portion of a farmer's income (or even all of it), may come from subsidies in certain years.

The subsidy programs and the establishment of national grasslands from federally purchased tracts of prairie in the 1930s hinted at a new era of managed use. In the 80 years hence there has not been another dust bowl.

On the other hand the massive reduction of plains biodiversity that began in the 1800s has continued unabated. A recent long term study of breeding bird species showed significant population declines in 24 of 28 species. The cause was habitat loss from clearing even more land for farming. The removal of trees and native grasses at the edges of fields and along highway corridors also leaves less room for wildlife, a big factor in the recent collapse of monarch butterfly populations. The damming and channelizing of rivers, along with intensive use of fertilizers, pesticides, and herbicides imperil fish populations.

During the last century 39 prairie animal and plant species were listed as endangered. This included the wolf and grizzly bear which had made a modest recovery by moving to the fringes of the plains from the Rocky Mountains. Among the listed birds are the piping plover, the peregrine falcon, and the whooping crane. The causes are varied, but behind them all is habitat loss-the conversion of a diverse prairie ecosystem into plowed fields of corn and soybeans.

The plight of the black-footed ferret is illustrative of ecosystem complexity. The ferret has not been a target of man’s extermination programs, but its main food source, the prairie dog, has been deliberately decimated. In the early 1900s prairie dogs numbered about five billion. Today, because of habitat loss and poisoning, the species occupies only about two percent of its former range. As a consequence, the black-footed ferret is one of the most endangered species in the world.

 

The greater prairie chicken is doing well in the Sandhills of central Nebraska. The lesser prairie chicken is struggling to maintain a presence in other parts of the prairie because of habitat loss.
