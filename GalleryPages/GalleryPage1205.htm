The birds and the bees are important.
G. Bynum-first grade student

Across the U.S. and around the world pollinators are in deep trouble, some at risk of extinction. There is much talk and hand-wringing, and almost no action over this crisis. This is not a "sweep under the rug" issue we can ignore until it goes away. How serious it it? Forests, prairies, wetlands, croplands, and every other terrestrial habitat on the planet depend on a healthy pollinator community to keep us and our animal friends well fed and healthy. Globally about 85% of all flowering plants require assistance from the birds and bees etc to produce seeds and fruit. Without them the earth's biodiversity will plummet.

The players in this high stakes game are well known. They include bats, hummingbirds, songbirds, and other vertebrates. But the key players are small, the spineless bees and others that zip around the backyard, and in the face. With a few exceptions (butterflies) we label them pests and spray them with poison. Maybe we should rethink this approach.

Agriculture depends heavily on managed European honeybees. Colonies are trucked from farm to farm like migrant workers to pollinate a hundred or so commercial crops, including fruits, vegetables, nuts, and more. There are about three million of these colonies in the U.S. alone and they contribute more than $24 billion to our nation's economy, not to mention health, each year. There's an old saying that you can thank a honeybee for one out of every three bites you eat!

The crisis began in 2006. "Migrant" worker bees leaving the colony for a day on the farm stopped returning to hive. The queen was abandoned by her subjects in this "rapture of the bees." Losses from 30-90% were reported by beekeepers in the first few years of what came to be called colony collapse disorder. Farmers and beekeepers flocked to Washington to tell lawmakers of their plight. They predicted a tenfold increase in food prices if the honeybee crisis persisted. In the ten years since this well-publicized problem began to unfold, Washington has done nothing.

How could this have happened? To start with we are dousing our country with agrochemicals produced by giant companies like Bayer and Monsanto, including neonicotinoids, or "neonics." Prior to their release the companies undertook multimillion dollar marketing campaigns to assure the EPA, Congress, and the public that their chemicals were safe for humans and did not harm non-targeted species. This hype was swallowed hook, line, and sinker. And the result-colony collapse disorder, farmlands converted to agri-deserts, billions of dollars in profit for corporate America, and no telling how many politicians in company pockets.

There are many confounding variables in the bee story such as mites, viruses, and loss of foraging habitat (from the use of Roundup, another Monsanto poison). But the bottom line is until we turn back the tidal wave of bee killing pesticides unleashed in this country in recent years, there will not be a recovery. Europe is already doing so.
