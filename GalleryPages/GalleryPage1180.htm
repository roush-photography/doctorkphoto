We are all guilty of subjectivity, or bias, even the "cold-blooded" scientist. It's only human. All of us look for, and "see" only evidence that confirms what we already believe. The difference is that scientific data are subjected to peer review and replication before acceptance as "truth." The lay opinion, often based on whim or junk science, is not subjected to scrutiny. We're free to believe what we want. And to complicate matters, even scientific "truth" can be short lived. Vitamin X is good for you today, bad for you tomorrow. This is a source of constant consternation to the public, yet there is no shame in changing one's mind if new data demand it. Uncertainty is inevitable on the front lines of any search for truth, but science will ultimately triumph.

Recently the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change of the United Nations, consisting of hundreds of climate scientists from around the world, released a fifth report in 25 years. In it they proclaimed, more emphatically than ever, that global warming is a fact, and that man has contributed substantially to it in the last half of the 20th century by burning fossil fuels. And yet, despite almost universal agreement by the experts (95%), half of all Americans still proudly proclaim their disbelief in either issue. This is significantly more than in other "advanced" countries.

Much research has been done in the past 25 years, most recently out of Yale University, in an effort to explain why Americans are so good at "burying their heads in the sand." Simplistically, it seems we fall into one of two tribes. The first believes that industry often behaves badly and must be regulated by government. The second believes that government is the problem and should stop meddling in private affairs. Whichever tribe we belong to we want very badly to be accepted.

So it would seem that peer pressure does not fade with high school graduation at all. It is alive and well at the club, at church, in local bars, and on the golf course. To remain in the graces of our group we will turn a blind eye to science if necessary, especially when it doesn't appear to cause any immediate harm. So we say no to climate change because the urgency is hard to grasp, the effects not immediately apparent. And we can even change our minds later if we need to.

But these really are not trivial issues. The earth's average temperature has increased, in step with rising atmospheric carbon dioxide levels. Glaciers are melting and sea levels are rising. So climate change is real and we are the major cause. Evolution is no longer a theory. It is fact. The evidence for this is incontrovertible. Vaccines are a good thing and the moon rocks are not fake. Being right matters and science has a long track record of getting things right in the end. Science has transported us to the outer reaches of the solar system, given us push button comfort and communication, and nearly doubled our life expectancy, all in a single century. Enough said.

Nevertheless, research is both flawed and biased, the truth elusive. Therefore, this volume, in the words of Ansel Adams, is my "aggressive personal opinion" based on 50 years of exploration and study. It is not necessarily a logical presentation of facts since only mathematics can do that. Conclusions are subject to change as new information becomes available.

 

The roseate spoonbill was on a fast track to extinction only a few years ago. The science of ecology, developed only in the last century, taught us the importance of habitat preservation to species viability. When we set aside nature reserves with intact ecosystems we are astonished at the resilience of most species. The above spoonbill and her kind are doing fine now, simply because we saved them a place.
