I want to sweep aside the dark gauze that covers the sea
and show people a world barely imaginable to them.
David Doubilet (Photographer)

Along with overpopulation, climate change, and the threat of thermonuclear war, the persistent decline of the world's oceans ranks among the great global crises confronting the human race today. But this huge threat to planet earth receives only a fraction of the "press" as the loss of tropical rain forests or charismatic megafauna. These are part of our vivid sensory experience, while life in the ocean can seem almost imaginary, hid beneath a "dark gauze" at the surface, as Doubilet so eloquently states. Out of sight, out of mind. And yet our future depends on whether or not we take notice of this problem and begin to do something about it.

A few facts about our oceans: 1) they contain 80% of all life on the planet 2) they provide half the oxygen in the atmosphere 3) oceans sequester five times more carbon than tropical rain forests 4) they create and stabilize our climate to a large extent 5) ten percent of people make their livings from the oceans 6) about $25 billion of illegal stock is removed from the oceans every year 7) coral reefs and estuaries, equivalent in productivity and diversity to tropical rain forests, are under serious threat.

It makes no difference whether you live in coastal Oregon or landlocked Kansas, the impact of oceans on the earth's ecological systems knows no boundaries. For our sake and that of unborn generations, indeed for the sake of all life, we must restore the health of the planet's oceans. Nothing we can do, short of avoiding nuclear war, will have a greater impact on the survival of life on earth. And no task before us will require more courage, sacrifice, and true leadership.
