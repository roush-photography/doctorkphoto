The sea has become the universal sewer.
Jacque Cousteau

Around the world oceans are a shell of their past. From the top of Greenland to the abyssal bottom of Antarctica we are seining our seas empty of fish. During the past 50 years populations of many commercially important species have declined by half or more. Half of what we eat from the sea today is farmed.

A recent report on the size of the world's fishing armada is telling. Some 37,000 industrial sized fishing ships plus about a million smaller boats are harvesting twice as many fish as just 25 years ago. Some trawlers are as big as cruise ships and drag nets behind that can hold a dozen 747 jumbo jets.

Perhaps the grimmest example of our plundering the seas took place off the coast of Newfoundland. The Grand Banks were described by British explorer John Cabot, in the late 1400s, as so bounteous with codfish that "one could walk across the ocean on their backs." And yet the world's most prolific fishery was barren by 1992, a fishing moratorium declared. As of 2016 that moratorium is still in place, with no recovery in sight. So fish sticks are no longer cod, but whatever is left. The backbone of the Newfoundland economy has crashed, perhaps for good.

In the 1970s large numbers of a little known fish were found off the coast of New Zealand. They were orange roughy and they were delicious. So you can imagine the end to this story. Roughy were hauled out of the ocean by the boatloads and shipped around the world. And then one day a scientist reported some alarming findings. These fish were long lived, up to 150 years, and slow maturing. They weren't up to the fishing pressure, and soon they too were gone. A moratorium on orange roughy fishing is currently in place in New Zealand, and as with cod elsewhere, there is no recovery in sight.

And the list goes on since there is money to be made and an exploding number of mouths to feed. A few more examples: 1) Bluefin tuna, king of the deep and prince of Japanese sushi, is in serious decline. 2) Pristine reefs off Indonesia (some of the few remaining in the world) are being poisoned by cyanide and blasted by dynamite to supply colorful hor d'oeuvres to China's burgeoning middle class. 3) In New England it was once routine to haul in lobsters weighing 20 pounds. Now it is rare to find one over two. 4) An estimated 100 million sharks are caught annually to make fin soup in China, Hong Kong, and Singapore. The dorsal fins are sliced off and the shark tossed back in the ocean to die a slow death from starvation. The nutritional value of shark fin is zilch and the soup's flavor come from seasoning. You can buy a bowl for about $100.

Greedy and ignorant seem a harsh indictment of the once noble fishing profession, but what else is there in light of the facts? But is this picture inevitable in a world bloated with hungry humans? Perhaps not. There are well managed fisheries in Alaska and Iceland to serve as examples. And New Zealand, much to their credit, learned a lesson from the roughy. As a result of mandated "No Fish" zones many stocks are recovering (not the slow growing roughy).

 

McWay Falls along the Big Sur coast of California. A few miles up the coast are Carmel, Monterey, San Francisco, and tens of thousands of rusted drums offshore that are leaking, or one day will be leaking, radioactive waste.
