To love a swamp is to love what is muted and marginal,
What exists in the shadow, what scurries along the damp edges.
Barbara Hurd

It's early A.M. and already warm and muggy. I am an hour out of New Orleans, cruising I-10 across University Lake in Baton Rouge and watching the morning parade go by. Soon I pass the nation's tallest state capitol building. My pace remains steady, though, for I have no business in the city of my birth. My destination is farther west, in Acadie (ah-cah-dee'), land of the Cajuns. Family and friends have been notified and I have my passport, just in case.

Past the Mississippi River and many miles of sugar cane I begin crossing the most expensive 30 miles of interstate ever constructed, a bridge over Atchafalaya, largest fresh water swamp in the United States. The sun sparkles o'er the tops of black willow and bald cypress. I gaze south at a watery blackness that stretches 50 miles to the Gulf of Mexico and ponder how eerily beautiful this yawning expanse really is.

Finally, after nearly an hour over swamp, terra firma reappears. At the whistle-stop of Cecilia I pull over for provisions at a small grocery. Inside the dusty walls are adorned with swamp critters that have seen better days and faded black and white photos of people devouring piles of crawfish, the "mud bug" that is one of many Cajun legends. About a dozen locals, speaking Creole French among other tongues, are being served hot boudin, the uniquely Cajun sausage. I take a place in line, receive the blessing, then speed away with my spicy links. Farther down the road my enthusiasm is fueled by the sounds of live Zydeco on Breaux Bridge radio, informing me that the annual Crawfish Festival has begun. It is 9A.M. and time to party.

I follow the music to David (dah veed) Olinde's Lounge. Inside the band is blaring and about 20 couples are doing an ad hoc two step on tiny parcels of dance floor. Pressed against the walls are more people, drinking, smoking, and clapping to the raunchy rhythms. Through the smoky haze I can make out a sign-"Smoking Only!" The sing-along is in French but I have no trouble translating: Laissez les bon temps roulez! (Let the good times roll!).

 

Despite decades of organized effort to assimilate Cajuns into American culture, south Louisiana remains a gloriously unique and culturally diverse region. Here you will find French, Spanish, West African, Caribbean, and many others blended with Acadian. This is perhaps most apparent in the food and language. Where else would you have a beignet for breakfast, a bowl of gumbo for lunch, and jambalaya for supper prior to an evening of Zydeco at the fais do-do?
