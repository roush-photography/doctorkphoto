For a century and a half photography has opened our eyes wider to the beauty and wonder of the world. It has permitted us to see things we could not before the frozen image, for example, a horse suspended in midair at full gallop. Photography has expanded the concept of aesthetics by teaching us that there is beauty in most everything. Items once considered mundane and not worth the artist's effort have been elevated to art with a camera: a dew drop, an autumn leaf, a single blossom illuminated by a godbeam. We can capture different viewpoints, from the microscope to the telescope, and even the satellite, and archive them for posterity. What a thrill it must have been for the old photographers to contemplate going beyond the paintbrush and actually freezing the real thing, creating a permanent record of people and the world. It must have seemed a miracle back then. How far we've come, yet it still does.

Given its widespread application, it is hard to overstate the influence of photography on modern life, from selling widgets to stopping wars. Few would argue with a suggestion that the camera is more powerful than the paintbrush or musical note, especially when one considers that television and movies are merely moving pictures.

Just as important, photography has become a valued part of most lives, not just the artistic few. Surveys show that 95 percent of homes in the U.S. have cameras or smartphones used to take pictures. Thus each of us can preserve memories to revisit privately, to share with family and friends, or to post on a website and make a statement. And how do you put a price on laughter, love, or personal satisfaction?
