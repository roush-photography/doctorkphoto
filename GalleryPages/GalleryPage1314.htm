Architecture presents the photographer with an opportunity to practice "seeing" the elements of graphic design: line, shape, form, texture, and pattern. These elements are present throughout nature, too, if you look for them. They are among the most important components of composition, most often supporting the subject, as in this cityscape, though they occasionally serve as primary subjects, in which case the work is considered abstract.

On a trip with a National Geographic group to circumnavigate the Baltic Sea, we spent some time in the former Soviet republic of Latvia, my favorite of the nine countries we visited. Gazing down this alley in Riga, the quaint capital city, I was impressed by the number and variety of geometric shapes and designs (count them-see if you can find more than 10). Even the wires (lines) are important to the photo graphics. The morning light made the buildings positively glow, while the deep shadows of doors and windows gave the whole scene a rich texture. I had framed the composition and was about to trip the shutter when these two ladies, stepping in sync, rounded the corner. Referred to in photography as "the moment," this was an unexpected gift that made a good picture suddenly memorable. Other examples would be a smile, a smirk, a touch, or a glance. Voila!
