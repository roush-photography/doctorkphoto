From the dawn of civilization religious and philosophical thought has had a major influence on the way man perceives the world. But a new force emerged about two centuries ago-the ideas of economics-which has come to profoundly influence, and some would say dominate, our worldview and social structure.

The first scholar to analyze this new wave of thought was Englishman Adam Smith in his On the Wealth of Nations, published in 1776. He proposed that individuals, acting in their own self-interest as producers and consumers, would lead to the best outcome for society at large. He shared the common eighteenth century belief in the inevitability of progress and saw society as engaged in a process of continual improvement through accumulation of individual wealth (a break from earlier, more pessimistic, Judeo-Christian thought). At the core of his philosophy was the concept of a self-regulating market with prices set by competition and a balance between supply and demand. Smith's ideas came to be known as classical or "free market" economics, and have become deeply ingrained in modern industrial societies.

The concept of centrally planned economies represented a counter to Smith's free market. In the twentieth century most nations turned to one of these systems. There was common ground, primarily the worship of progress through economic growth, but a radical difference in how this would be achieved. The major difference was in how economic decisions would be made. In a pure socialist economy the government owns and controls the means of production, distribution, and exchange. Marx and Lenin, the system's founder and principal proponent, believed that it would be more efficient at meeting the real needs of people through decisions made by their wise leaders. It is a system of fairness-"from each according to his abilities and to each according to his needs"-at least in theory. During the 20th century some viewed Marxism as the world's greatest hope, while others thought it the greatest threat.

 

For nearly all of the last century the Soviet Union was the world's most powerful communist country and centrally planned economy. It was a union of 15 republics, including Ukraine (photograph), and one of two superpowers that stood eyeball-to-eyeball during the Cold War. The dramatic collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 is well known. Sadly this has led to economic chaos in most of the nations that were once part of that union. There are only five remaining communist countries in the world (China, Laos, Vietnam, Cuba, North Korea) and most are slowly converting to free market economies. Vietnam, in fact, has one of the fastest growing economies in the world since encouraging private ownership of industry, commerce, and agriculture. (next photo)
