One of the major flaws in all economic systems is that the earth's resources are treated only as capital-assets to be turned into profit. Trees, wildlife, soil, minerals, and water are just commodities, or economic goods, their price the cost of extracting and turning them into something that will sell. More importantly, economic systems assume, in defiance of all logic, that the earth's resources are inexhaustible, and that economic growth can continue forever. And since economics also asserts that the most rational action for an individual is to vigorously pursue his immediate self-interest, the future is ignored.

But nature does more for us than just provide lumber, steel, bush meat, and so forth for human needs. Natural ecosystems maintain the very life support systems of our planet. Yet those of us in the developed world seldom give these services much thought. So our clean air and water, soil formation, oxygen balance, pollination, and all the rest are taken for granted as we single-mindedly pursue the almighty dollar like good free market soldiers. This is a serious mistake.

In 1997 a team of 13 scientists and economists collaborated on a first-ever study to place a dollar value on the services ecosystems perform for us (and all life, for that matter). They identified 17 major goods and services we depend on and calculated a dollar value for each. The total value came to $41 trillion, considered a conservative estimate. To put this value in perspective, the total world GDP in 2010 was $60 trillion.

From a different perspective, we are talking about the aggregate value of life on earth, surely beyond dollars and cents . Given the essential nature of ecosystems, we, as the only stewards of our planet, must assure their health and survival. Only in this manner can we too survive.

 

Land, sea, and sky. The dramatic western coast of Ireland, the Emerald Isle, is one of my favorites of the many countries I have visited. During three weeks of exploring, I fell spectacularly and permanently in love with the place. But there is trouble in paradise (beyond the constant interfaith haggling). Centuries ago, 80 percent of Ireland was covered by native forests. Because of unsustainable logging after the arrival of Europeans, that number is now less than 1 percent, making Ireland the most deforested of all European nations. In the meantime the island nation's fisheries are suffering the same fate, as stocks have critically declined due to overfishing. And neither have the skies been spared. There are far fewer breeding birds here than in nearby countries because of habitat degradation. Thus, while many species have been recorded, the majority are rare or migratory, and spring is mostly silent.
