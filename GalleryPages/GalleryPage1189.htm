The environmental movement is less than 60 years old but its roots date back to the late nineteenth century. The story begins with the tales of four species.

The year 2014 marked the centennial commemoration of the extraordinary extinction of the passenger pigeon. For centuries this pigeon was believed to be indestructible, the most abundant bird in North America, and probably the world, with numbers estimated in the billions. Yet this remarkably successful species was extinguished in just three decades by human exploitation. The last documented passenger pigeon in the wild was in 1900, in Ohio. Martha, the sole surviving captive bird, lived an additional 14 years at the Cincinnati Zoo. On September 2nd, at the ripe old age of 17, she died, one of the few extinctions ever to be witnessed "live."

But Martha would not go quietly. This extinction caused reverberations. She and her kind became a rallying cry for a budding new morality in this country that believed it was just wrong for man to indiscriminately annihilate a species for fun and profit. The passenger pigeon became a symbol of staggering abundance on the one hand and human greed and callousness on the other.

 

This picture is a good example of storytelling in photography. At first glance it is a happy story. It shows a moose and calf wading the Colorado River near its source in the Rocky Mountains. Public lands, such as national parks, national forests, and others have helped to keep this nation "green" and biodiverse, with many threatened species returning from the brink. For example, the moose was extinct in Colorado until its reintroduction only recently. With protection numbers have increased rapidly, and there is now a limited hunting season.

But a closer look at this photograph reveals a not-so-happy side to the story. The devastating effects of the pine bark beetle are obvious in the background. Virtually all lodgepole pine trees in Rocky Mountain National Park have succumbed to the effects of the beetle. This is due to multiple changes in the environment brought on by global warming. The infestation is expected to effect all species of conifers (spruce, fir, and pine) in the Rocky Mountains, from Alaska to Mexico. The worst prognosis is near 100 percent mortality of every species during the next few decades. (next photo)
