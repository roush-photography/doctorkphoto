Easter Island was a cultural "hotspot" when the rest of the world was wallowing in the Dark Ages (the 1600s). Its people were well fed, society tightly organized, and religion highly sophisticated. Society centered on a dozen or so clans, each ruled by a chief. Since food, mostly sweet potatos and chickens, was easily attained, there was ample time for matters of the spirit. A complex form of ancestor worship evolved. Ancestors were believed to provide health and prosperity for the islanders in exchange for offerings and ceremony.

Each clan, at the behest of its chief, constructed ceremonial platforms called ahu upon which they erected giant statues (above). These statues represented incarnations of ancestral chiefs and were extremely important in clan worship. But it was the carving, and especially the transport of the massive moai (largest above weighs 86 tons), which played such a pivotal role in the story of Easter Island.

The islanders had only human power to drag the statues along, using tree trunks as rollers. As the population increased, clans competed to carve the most, and the biggest, statues. Ultimately, there were hundreds of ceremonial platforms and over 800 statues on their tiny singularity.

Then, when the society was at its peak, something mysterious happened. Hundreds of statues were abandoned, along transport roads and at various stages of completion at the quarry. A civilization came to a halt. How? Why? Theories abound-perhaps civil war or genocide? But the major force behind the falling star of Polynesia was environmental degradation brought on by massive deforestation of the entire island. Not a single tree was left standing! Gone with the trees were boats (and thus the means to fish or escape), fuel, and constuction material. And without tree roots there was soil erosion, and thus fewer sweet potatos.

But just as important, without trees for transport there was no reason to carve statues. Thus they had no work, as there was little else to do on the island. The statues were also the backbone of their religious rituals and ceremonial customs. So too their spirit world collapsed. Therefore in the end there was no food, no fuel, no work, no religion, and no escape.

The story of Easter Island presents in stark relief, what happens when we lay waste the world around us. Their social order fell with the last tree, and the islanders became mired in the drawn out paroxysms of societal and environmental collapse.
