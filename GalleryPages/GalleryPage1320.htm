The biggest problem confronting the environmental activist is the power of money.
Jane Goodall

In a pure free market economy, demand-not the wise and benevolent leader(s)-determines what will be exchanged. Goods and services are offered in a market that is free from government interference. The market is self-regulated by competition and the law of supply and demand. If supply is less than demand prices naturally rise, whereas if there is an oversupply, prices fall. The whole system is private and driven by the desire of people to acquire personal wealth.

In practice, both centrally planned and free market economies have many flaws, and there is not a pure form of either system anywhere. The developed countries all utilize free market economies, but government regulation occurs on many fronts, more so in some of the "social democracies" of Western Europe. This might consist of adjusting interest rates, lowering taxes, or increased government spending to stimulate a sluggish economy. Both systems have been widely criticized for lacking a "conscience," by government in the case of socialism and by big business in the case of capitalism. The outcome is the same: exploitation of the working class in the ruthless pursuit of personal wealth by those in power, the so-called class struggle.

Human societies and their economic activities often do great harm to the environment, and that has a direct impact on human welfare, immediately and in the future. The effects of pollution and misuse of resources can be clearly seen in developing countries, where little in the way of environmental public policy is in place. Widespread disease and death can be directly traced to degraded environments. The cost to human welfare is felt not only in the area of health, but in economic productivity and social stability. Laws to protect the environment are not luxuries to be tolerated only if they don't interfere with economic growth. They are a keystone in the foundation of organized human society.

 

Bhutan is a Buddhist monarchy widely acclaimed as one of the more "enlightened" countries of our time. Rather strange kudos for a tiny, poor, and globally insignificant nation that most outsiders have never heard of. There are, in fact, many reasons for this unexpected recognition. To list a few: 1) National success is measured by GDH, or gross domestic happiness, not by economic growth as elsewhere. 2) Smoking was recently banned, the only country to do so. 3) All agriculture is organic by law. 4) Bhutan's carbon footprint is nil. 5) According to the International Union For Conservation of Nature Bhutan is a role model for proactive conservation. Nearly 60 percent of the country is protected in preserves. The next closest is Costa Rica at 25 percent (the U.S. is about 6 percent). 6) The United Nations calls Bhutan an inspiration for how countries and economies can address climate change and still improve the lives of their people.
