The planet groans every time it registers another birth.
Paul Simon

In his quest to create heaven on earth man has brought himself to the brink of hell. He has swarmed the earth like locusts, trashed it, destroyed much of the tropical forest, polluted every sphere, sucked rivers and aquifers dry, depleted the "boundless" oceans, damaged half the world's coral reefs, produced hundreds of thousands of weapons of mass destruction, and changed the planet's thermostat. And he's not done yet.

Our species and planet earth are at a critical juncture. Over the next 25 years human population will increase to nine billion. Perhaps a more telling statistic is that three billion of those will be middle class consumers. This will place unprecedented demands on the earth for food, water, and energy, expected to nearly double for all three in this short time frame. At some point the question must be asked-how much water, food, and energy can the planet provide over the long haul? One thing is certain. There is a limit.

And then there are the environmental concerns. Traditionally threats to the environment have been looked upon as isolated, and potentially preventable, events such as war, the nuclear meltdown at Chernobyl, and BP's Deep Water Horizon oil spill. These are still major concerns. But the biggest threat to the environment now is generalized degradation from the necessary day to day economic activities of our expanding population (agriculture, industry, manufacturing, urban development etc). And these are definitely not preventable. Economic growth is a good measure of our impact on the earth's limited resources, and total world GDP is expected to increase from $60 trillion in 2010 to nearly $100 trillion by 2020. Can we suvive our own success?

And yet there seems reason for hope. Our species has a long history of coming back from the brink. And advances in science and technology have given us an extraordinary understanding of the dilemmas we face and what to do about them. The state of our world over the next several decades will largely depend on whether we have the political courage to do what is necessary to apply this knowledge.

 

The Woodstock community of Vermont is noted as a hamlet of the rich and famous. Middle and upper classes consume a disproportionate share of the world's resources. For example, the United States, with 5% of the world's population, consumes 40% of the world's petroleum.
