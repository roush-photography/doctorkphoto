For 25 years I chased light and subject across 50 states, initially driven more to explore and document than to save the world. Somewhere along the way, an uneasy feeling began to nag at me as I witnessed first hand the environmental issues that made headlines during the sixties. I became increasingly aware of threats to wildlife and wild places from human encroachment. Nature was under siege. I learned that we were in the midst of the greatest extinction crisis the world had ever known, and that the cause was human impact on our planet. I watched in dismay as the wild edge of my neighborhood was replaced by urban sprawl, subdivisions multiplying like cancer. These realizations tore at my heart even as I continued to record the wonders that remained.

Consider the changes in one lifetime. In 1948, the year I was born, Gatlinburg, Tennessee was a backwoods hamlet that boasted a single retail outlet, Ogle's General Store. Today the place is choked with sightseers, smog, and shops selling souvenirs made in China. What's left of the former hamlet is buried under a parking lot (Joni Mitchell was right..). All of those cars spell bumper-to-bumper traffic, from downtown to the top of Clingman's Dome. In the meantime, at Pigeon Forge, just down the road a piece from Gatlinburg, things are worse! Thank you, Dollywood. The traffic may not move for hours, and there are even more parking lots. And the most shocking thing about all of this is that no one is shocked about it. The hyperbole and oversell of the 21st century is now the norm.

Inevitably, the focus of my travels shifted from local to global, with an idea of providing a window to the world through which others could share my vision, and maybe even my concern. We live in a wondrous world, a spherical garden teeming with life. Viewed from space, our planet sparkles like a blue-and-white marble afloat in a barren universe. There are landscapes of infinite variety and a treasure trove of wildlife that need protection. Over time, I came to the conclusion that we must somehow find the will to conserve a representative sampling of the earth's biodiversity before it is too late-the conservationist's vision of Noah's ark. Many scientists have stated that the minimum necessary is 15 percent of each ecosystem. This is no less a goal than saving our planet. I decided to use my travel photography to help make it happen.

 

A recent international trip was to South Africa, which featured a spectacular show by a pod of several hundred common dolphins in False Bay, near Cape Town.
